The subject of agitation appears in the poem that became "Myself and Mine": "Let others praise eminent men and hold up peace—I hold up agitation and conflict" (1860, p. 224).

The words "emptier of privies" from these lines are echoed in the poem eventually titled "Song of Myself": "To a drudge of the cottonfields or emptier of privies I lean" (1855, p. 45). These lines also match the following lines from the poem: "Long have you timidly waded, holding a plank by the shore, / Now I will you to be a bold swimmer, / To jump off in the midst of the sea, and rise again and nod to me and shout, and laughingly dash with your hair" (1855, p. 52).

Lines from this section appear, revised, in the poem that became "Song of the Answerer": "He says indifferently and alike, How are you friend? to the President at his levee, / And he says Good day my brother, to Cudge that hoes in the sugarfield;" (1855, p. 86).

Lines from this section correspond to a line from the manuscript poem, unpublished in Whitman's lifetime, titled "Pictures": "And now a merry recruiter passes, with fife and drum, seeking who will join his troop." The first several lines of the poem (not including this line) were revised and published in The American in October 1880 as "My Picture-Gallery," a poem later included in Leaves of Grass as part of the "Autumn Rivulets" cluster (1881, p. 310).
